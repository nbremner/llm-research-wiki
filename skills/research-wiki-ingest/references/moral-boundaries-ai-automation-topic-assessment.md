# Moral boundaries of AI automation topic assessment

Use this pattern when ingesting sources that distinguish AI resistance based on social, moral, cultural, identity, dignity, care, or human-presence objections rather than ordinary performance, trust, usability, literacy, or access barriers.

## Create/promote signal

Promote or update `moral-boundaries-of-ai-automation` when the source's central construct is a principled boundary around AI replacement: AI may be technically capable, cheaper, or accurate, but people still judge automation as inappropriate because the role or task carries care, dignity, spiritual authority, moral responsibility, relational presence, or symbolic human meaning.

This should not be flattened into:

- `ai-adoption` when the mechanism is not access, readiness, discovery, training, image concern, or fit.
- `automation-and-substitution` when the contribution is a social-feasibility constraint on substitution rather than technical or economic substitution evidence.
- `ai-receptivity` when the attitude is not generic willingness to use AI but a boundary between acceptable assistance and unacceptable replacement.
- `ai-workforce-impact-measurement` when the paper contributes a measurement input but the central claim is moral/social acceptability.

## Distinctions to preserve

- **Performance-based resistance**: expected to recede as AI improves in accuracy, reliability, cost, speed, or bias.
- **Principle-based resistance**: persists even when AI is stipulated to perform better or cheaper.
- **Assistance vs. replacement**: people may accept AI helping humans while rejecting AI fully replacing humans in the same occupation.
- **Public/social feasibility vs. realized adoption**: public moral repugnance is evidence about social acceptability, not direct evidence of employer behavior, customer demand, regulation, professional governance, wages, hiring, or realized substitution.
- **Occupation-level morality vs. task-level design**: moral boundaries may attach to recognized social roles even though AI enters through task components and backstage workflows.

## Typical topic updates

- `moral-boundaries-of-ai-automation`: main synthesis home; define the boundary and contradictions.
- `automation-and-substitution`: add that substitution forecasts need a moral/social-feasibility layer beyond capability and cost.
- `ai-adoption`: add principle-based adoption constraints as distinct from mapping/readiness/literacy/image concerns.
- `ai-workforce-impact-measurement`: add social acceptability as a separate measurement input, with limits.
- `overview.md`: add or update thin-area/open-question language about whether moral boundaries translate into real organizational, regulatory, or labor-market outcomes.

## Caution

Do not overclaim durable protection. Moral repugnance may protect some roles from AI automation, but future evidence must show whether public attitudes affect employer adoption, professional norms, law, customer acceptance, or labor demand. Surface inequality implications if protected roles map unevenly onto wage, race, gender, or occupational-status patterns.
