# Behavioral human-centered AI topic assessment

Use this note when ingesting sources about AI adoption as behavioral change management, human-centered AI in organizations, or behavioral decision mechanisms in AI implementation.

## Trigger pattern

Promote or update `[[behavioral-human-centered-ai]]` when the source's central contribution is not just "AI adoption" or "responsible AI" but the behavioral layer beneath implementation: bounded rationality, designer bias, algorithm aversion, loss aversion, availability, autonomy threat, trust calibration, overconfidence, escalation of commitment, or other predictable human reactions shaping AI design, adoption, and management.

## Topic boundary

`[[behavioral-human-centered-ai]]` should remain a peer topic, not a dumping ground for all adoption sources.

- Use `[[ai-adoption]]` when the main mechanism is access, use-case discovery, observed use, task characteristics, receptivity, image concerns, or whether AI is used at all.
- Use `[[ai-readiness]]` when the main mechanism is organizational capacity: strategic alignment, resources, knowledge, culture, data, governance, managerial capabilities, or workforce capability.
- Use `[[responsible-ai-deployment]]` when the main mechanism is governance, accountability, evidence thresholds, worker protections, fairness, privacy, appeal, monitoring, or stakeholder voice.
- Use `[[human-ai-collaboration]]` when the main mechanism is task-level authority, review, trust calibration, delegation, stewardship, coordination, or human accountability inside the workflow.
- Use `[[behavioral-human-centered-ai]]` when the source explicitly argues that AI systems and AI implementation must be designed around real human behavior rather than rational-user, rational-designer, or rational-leader assumptions.

## Source pattern from Schweitzer et al. (2025)

`[[2025-schweitzer-leading-ai-adoption]]` justified creating the topic because it made a class-level conceptual contribution: AI adoption in organizations should be treated as behavioral human-centered change management across three stakeholder/stage groups.

- **Design stage:** designers can be affected by confirmation bias, inventor's bias, and anchoring on technical specifications or KPIs, producing systems that miss usability, acceptance, fairness, or well-being.
- **Adoption stage:** organizational members can overweigh vivid AI failures, job-loss narratives, safety concerns, surveillance concerns, autonomy loss, or threats to skill value.
- **Management stage:** leaders can overestimate their ability to oversee AI implementation or escalate commitment to ineffective/harmful AI investments because of sunk costs.

## Default integration pattern

For future similar sources:

1. Add the source to `[[behavioral-human-centered-ai]]` if it contributes a distinct behavioral mechanism, stage model, implementation principle, or empirical test.
2. Add only a concise pointer to `[[ai-adoption]]`, `[[ai-readiness]]`, or `[[responsible-ai-deployment]]` when the source changes those pages' synthesis; do not duplicate the full behavioral framework across all adjacent topics.
3. Preserve the governance tension: behavioral insight can make AI implementation more realistic and worker-centered, but it can also become persuasion to overcome resistance without redesigning harmful systems. Surface that tension under contradictions/open questions when relevant.
4. If a source is merely about AI acceptance, TAM/UTAUT, general trust, or receptivity without an explicit behavioral-HCAI or change-management contribution, consider `[[ai-receptivity]]`, `[[ai-adoption]]`, or a deferred watchlist item rather than expanding `[[behavioral-human-centered-ai]]`.

## Approval note

Creating or materially expanding `[[behavioral-human-centered-ai]]` is topic synthesis and requires owner approval before commit. Source records may still be auto-committed with existing-topic feeds; add the new topic feed only in the approved synthesis diff when the topic is new or unapproved.
