# Human-AI contracting / oversight incentives topic assessment

Use this note when ingesting sources about principal-agent models, incentive compatibility, contracting, compensation, liability, monitoring, or economic incentives for human oversight in AI-supported work.

## Pattern from Bastani & Cachon (2025)

`[[2025-bastani-human-ai-contracting-paradox]]` introduced a distinct mechanism: human-AI collaboration can be system-optimal while still unattractive to the principal because rare, unpredictable AI failures make human vigilance costly to incentivize. As AI reliability rises and errors become rarer, the wage/contract needed to keep humans inspecting and correcting low-probability failures may rise rather than fall.

## Topic-map guidance

- **Do not flatten this into generic human-AI collaboration.** The central construct is not only capability complementarity or interface fit; it is whether organizations can economically sustain the human effort that makes oversight real.
- **Update existing topics when the source adds incentive logic:**
  - `[[human-ai-collaboration]]` — collaboration requires incentive-compatible oversight, not only calibrated trust/review.
  - `[[automation-complacency]]` — rare failures can make vigilance expensive even without laziness or naive overtrust.
  - `[[human-ai-task-allocation]]` — allocation depends on whether hybrid oversight is contractible/economically sustainable, not only who is more accurate.
  - `[[work-redesign]]` — redesign needs compensation, monitoring, liability, escalation, and contract economics alongside task decomposition.
  - `[[automation-and-substitution]]` — substitution can follow from contracting failure, not just technical superiority or worker preference.
- **Defer/watchlist initially:** `ai-oversight-incentives / human-AI contracting` unless multiple sources add empirical or governance evidence. Promote when another clean source addresses compensation, monitoring, liability, or incentive design for human oversight.

## Wording guardrails

- Do not imply "worse AI is better" as a general claim. The precise claim is conditional: when human effort is endogenous and hard to observe, higher AI reliability can make low-probability oversight harder/more expensive to incentivize, and a principal may prefer a less reliable tool in some regimes.
- Keep theoretical model evidence separate from empirical field evidence. Treat analytical principal-agent models as mechanism-building, not proof of deployment outcomes.
- Surface the contradiction between system-level value and principal-level adoption incentives; do not resolve it away as mere complacency or adoption friction.
