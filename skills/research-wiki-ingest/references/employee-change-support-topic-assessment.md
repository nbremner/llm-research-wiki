# Employee change support topic-assessment pattern

Use this reference when ingesting sources about employee support for organizational change, change appraisal, resistance to change, compliance, championing, change management support, or discretionary advocacy during transformation.

## Core distinction

Do not flatten these sources into generic `ai-adoption`, `ai-readiness`, or `behavioral-human-centered-ai` just because the user frames them as relevant to AI transformation. The reusable construct is **employee change support**: the difference between minimal compliance and active championing, and the appraisal processes that move employees toward or away from each.

A source warrants or strengthens `employee-change-support` when it centrally addresses one or more of:

- compliance vs. championing / active support / discretionary advocacy;
- change-related threat vs. challenge appraisal;
- change management support, communication, mutual expectation clarity, or trust in management competence as predictors of support;
- dispositional resistance to change as a moderator, not just as generic resistance;
- how workers support, promote, repair, or socially legitimate transformation beyond formal policy adherence.

## Topic-map guidance

- **Create/update `employee-change-support`** when the source’s main contribution concerns support behavior during change or appraisal-mediated change response.
- **Also update `ai-adoption`** only when the source clarifies how adoption metrics can confuse compliance, usage, training completion, or policy adherence with active support/championing.
- **Also update `behavioral-human-centered-ai`** when the source gives mechanisms for bounded, affective, or appraisal-based reactions to AI-enabled change.
- **Also update `ai-readiness`** when the source frames change-support capacity as an organizational capability: credible information, expectation clarity, trust, leadership competence, and management support.
- **Defer separate topics** such as `change-appraisal`, `dispositional-resistance-to-change`, or `change-management-support` until several sources make one of those submechanisms central enough to stand alone.

## AI transformation framing

For AI transformation, the practical synthesis is: leaders cannot infer commitment from surface compliance. Employees may follow rollout instructions while withholding the discretionary sensemaking, peer advocacy, experimentation, and repair work that make AI-enabled change spread. Conversely, championing is not automatically good; employees can champion poorly governed AI systems, overstate benefits, or pressure peers into harmful adoption, so change support remains bounded by responsible deployment and evidence quality.

## Source pattern that triggered this reference

`2018-fugate-change-compliance-championing` (Fugate & Soenen, 2018) used a two-wave merger field study to show that change management support predicted lower threat appraisal and higher challenge appraisal; challenge appraisal predicted compliance and championing; threat appraisal predicted lower championing but not compliance; and change management support mattered especially for employees higher in dispositional resistance to change. Treat this as foundational non-AI evidence, not direct AI implementation evidence.
