# Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier: Field Experimental Evidence of the Effects of AI on Knowledge Worker Productivity and Quality

**Citation.** Dell'Acqua, F., McFowland III, E., Mollick, E., Lifshitz-Assaf, H., Kellogg, K. C., Rajendran, S., Krayer, L., Candelon, F., & Lakhani, K. R. (2023). *Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier: Field Experimental Evidence of the Effects of AI on Knowledge Worker Productivity and Quality.* Harvard Business School Working Paper 24-013. SSRN abstract 4573321.

**Summary.** A randomized field experiment with 758 Boston Consulting Group consultants tested GPT-4 assistance on realistic management consulting tasks. The paper introduces the "jagged technological frontier": AI helps on some knowledge-work tasks but harms performance on others that appear similarly difficult to humans. Within the frontier, AI improved productivity and quality; outside it, AI users were less likely to solve the task correctly. The core design implication is that AI adoption depends on mapping task-specific capability boundaries, not just giving workers access to tools.

## Key claims
- On 18 tasks selected to be inside the AI capability frontier, consultants using AI completed **12.2% more tasks**, completed them **25.1% more quickly**, and produced outputs rated **more than 40% higher quality** than the control group.
- AI assistance benefited consultants across the baseline-skill distribution: below-average performers improved **43%** relative to their own baseline scores, while above-average performers improved **17%**.
- On a complex task selected to be outside the frontier, consultants using AI were **19 percentage points less likely** to produce correct solutions than consultants without AI.
- The authors describe two successful human-AI work patterns: "Centaurs," who divided and delegated work between themselves and AI, and "Cyborgs," who tightly integrated AI throughout their task flow.
- The same knowledge-work domain can contain both AI-augmentable and AI-degrading tasks, making the practical adoption problem one of task diagnosis, workflow mapping, and calibrated human judgment.

## Evidence & limitations
- **Design:** randomized controlled laboratory-in-the-field experiment with 758 BCG consultants. Participants first completed baseline tasks, then were randomly assigned to no AI, GPT-4 access, or GPT-4 access plus a prompt-engineering overview. Tasks were realistic consulting exercises; AI interaction logs were captured through a GPT-4 platform.
- **Evidence strength:** strong causal design for short-run task performance among highly skilled consultants, with blind expert grading and quantitative productivity/quality outcomes.
- **Limitations:** working-paper version, though later publicly listed as published in *Organization Science*; conducted in one professional-services firm and one period of GPT-4 capability; frontier boundaries are task- and model-specific and may shift quickly; the results do not show long-run learning, deskilling, adoption durability, or organizational performance effects.
