# Designing the Post-Role Enterprise: Human-AI Orchestration, Fluid Skill Clusters and the end of the Job Description

**Citation.** Ermakov, A. (2026). *Designing the Post-Role Enterprise: Human-AI Orchestration, Fluid Skill Clusters and the end of the Job Description.* SSRN abstract 6438102.

**Summary.** Ermakov argues that enterprise AI adoption is blocked less by attitudes or tooling than by inherited role architecture: static job descriptions are poorly matched to agentic workflows where humans and AI systems jointly plan, execute, and adapt work. The paper proposes the Tri-Layer Orchestration Model — strategic intelligence, dynamic allocation, and execution/observability — as a conceptual framework for organizing work around fluid skill clusters, human-critical nodes, and accountability-by-design. The contribution is theoretical and practitioner-facing rather than empirical; its strongest value for the wiki is as an explicit architecture-of-work argument for the post-role enterprise.

## Key claims
- The paper frames the AI adoption gap as architectural: executives report high intent to redesign work for AI, but organizations lack the work design, governance, skills intelligence, and accountability infrastructure needed to convert experimentation into value.
- Static job descriptions encode an industrial-era assumption of stable tasks, role boundaries, and skill requirements; agentic AI undermines that assumption by shifting who or what performs cognitive work across workflows.
- The proposed Tri-Layer Orchestration Model has three linked layers: Strategic Intelligence, Dynamic Allocation, and Execution & Observability.
- “Fluid Skill Clusters” are dynamic assemblies of human competencies and digital labor capabilities organized around outcomes rather than fixed roles or permanent team membership.
- Human-Critical Nodes are points in workflows where consequences are severe, ethical/relational judgment is required, or visible human accountability is necessary; these should be designed explicitly before deployment rather than inferred after failure.
- Performance management in agentic workflows should shift from role-based evaluation toward cluster-level outcome tracking, human-agent workflow quality, and attribution across human and machine contributors.
- The CPO role is positioned as a potential human-digital orchestration function, accountable for integrating people strategy, skills intelligence, work design, and AI-enabled operating models.

## Evidence & limitations
- The paper is a conceptual framework and synthesis, drawing on Mercer Global Talent Trends 2026, McKinsey State of Organizations 2026 and agentic-organization materials, Egon Zehnder leadership research, socio-technical systems theory, and scientific management.
- It is not an empirical validation of the Tri-Layer Orchestration Model. The author explicitly notes that Human-Critical Node mapping, Performance Attribution protocols, and Skills Intelligence infrastructure remain design propositions rather than tested prescriptions.
- Public provenance needs confirmation because the SSRN landing page could not be independently extracted from this environment due to Cloudflare blocking.
- Treat as a useful conceptual architecture source, not as causal evidence.
