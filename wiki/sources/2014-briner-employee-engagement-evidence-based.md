# What is employee engagement and does it matter?: An evidence-based approach

**Citation.** Briner, R. B. (2014). *What is employee engagement and does it matter?: An evidence-based approach.* Invited paper for Engage for Success, The Future of Engagement thought piece collection.

**Summary.** Briner critiques the practitioner concept of employee engagement from an evidence-based practice perspective. The article argues that employee engagement lacks a stable definition, is often measured with overlapping satisfaction/commitment/effort items, and is frequently treated as new despite heavy conceptual overlap with older I-O constructs. Its central claim is not that engagement cannot matter, but that common engagement claims outrun the construct validity, measurement, and causal evidence available.

## Key claims
- Employee engagement has no agreed definition; Briner cites reviews finding more than 50 definitions, spanning behaviors, attitudes, feelings, work conditions, and causal combinations of these.
- Without definitional clarity, practitioners and researchers may believe they are discussing the same construct while actually measuring and acting on different things.
- Many engagement measures appear to combine job satisfaction, organizational commitment, effort, and related attitudes; Briner notes that Gallup's Q12 has been reported to correlate .91 with unit-level job satisfaction and .8 with organizational commitment.
- The popular HR practitioner concept of employee engagement should be distinguished from the academic construct of work engagement developed by Schaufeli, Bakker, and colleagues.
- Engagement may add little conceptual value if it mostly relabels older constructs such as job satisfaction, commitment, motivation, organizational identification, job involvement, positive affect, discretionary effort, and citizenship behavior.
- The most important practical questions are causal: whether increases in engagement cause increases in performance, and whether engagement interventions raise engagement and then performance. Briner argues that public evidence available at the time largely could not answer these causal questions.
- Engagement advocacy often over-claims by treating correlations as causal evidence or mis-claims by using evidence about other constructs as evidence for employee engagement.

## Evidence & limitations
- **Type:** invited practitioner-facing thought piece, not a new empirical study or systematic review.
- **Evidence base:** conceptual critique drawing on academic and practitioner literature on engagement, work engagement, job attitudes, performance, measurement, and evidence-based management.
- **Strength:** clear construct-validity and evidence-quality critique; useful for disciplining loose engagement claims in people analytics and organizational practice.
- **Limitations:** written in 2014, so it does not incorporate later evidence reviews or newer engagement/people analytics measurement work; it is argumentative rather than itself a formal evidence synthesis.
