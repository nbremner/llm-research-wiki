# Mapping AI into Production: A Field Experiment on Firm Performance

**Citation.** Kim, H., Kim, D., & Koning, R. (2026). *Mapping AI into Production: A Field
Experiment on Firm Performance.* INSEAD Working Paper 2026/20/STR. SSRN abstract 6513481.
Preregistered AEA RCT Registry AEARCTR-0016746.

**Summary.** A preregistered randomized field experiment across 515 high-growth startups in a
3-month INSEAD accelerator. All firms got identical AI tools, credits, and technical training; the
only treatment was case-study workshops showing how AI-native firms reorganize production. The paper
isolates what the authors call the **mapping problem** — discovering *where and how* AI creates value
inside a firm's production process — and shows that relieving it causally raises firm performance.
The binding constraint is managerial/cognitive search, not access to the technology.

## Key claims
- Treated firms discovered **44% more AI use cases** (8.8 vs 6.1; p<0.01), spread across more functions
  (+0.84 functional categories), concentrated in product development and strategy.
- They completed **~12% more tasks** (20.9 vs 18.5; p<0.05), driven almost entirely by **internal**
  tasks (+22%); external tasks unchanged.
- They were **18% more likely to acquire paying customers** (+11pp; p<0.01) and generated **1.9×
  higher revenue** (2.2× conditional on any revenue; p<0.05).
- Gains concentrate in the **upper tail (90th–95th pct)** — AI "lifts the ceiling," not the floor.
  No significant heterogeneity by baseline performance or technical-founder background.
- **Capital demand fell ~39.5% (~$220k; p<0.05) with labor demand unchanged** — "do more with less."
- IV/2SLS (treatment instrumenting AI use, first-stage F>33): each additional use case →
  +0.85 tasks, +3.9pp customer probability, +0.23 log revenue, −0.17 log capital demand.

## Evidence & limitations
- **Design:** preregistered RCT (515 firms, 255T/260C), stratified randomization, strata fixed
  effects, firm-clustered SEs; IV/2SLS for returns to AI use. Primary outcomes double-coded blind to
  condition. Low attrition (1.6%, balanced); sentiment checks rule out a Hawthorne/motivation story.
- **Limitations:** working paper, not peer-reviewed; short 3-month window (no evidence on whether
  gains compound or fade); several headline results are marginal (p<0.1); revenue effect rides on a
  few upper-tail "breakout" firms; early-stage, self-selected, AI-focused accelerator applicants —
  not representative of established firms. Authors flag 0→8.8 use-case extrapolations as "heroic."
