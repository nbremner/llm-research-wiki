# Novice risk work: How juniors coaching seniors on emerging technologies such as generative AI can lead to learning failures

**Citation.** Kellogg, K. C., Lifshitz, H., Randazzo, S., Mollick, E., Dell'Acqua, F., McFowland III, E., Candelon, F., & Lakhani, K. R. (2025). *Novice risk work: How juniors coaching seniors on emerging technologies such as generative AI can lead to learning failures.* Information and Organization, 35(1), 100559. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.infoandorg.2025.100559

**Summary.** This paper studies why junior professionals may fail to become reliable teachers for senior professionals learning generative AI. Based on interviews with 78 junior BCG consultants after a GPT-4 field experiment, the authors argue that juniors can recommend "novice AI risk mitigation tactics" that under-address emerging-technology risks. The paper shifts the explanation from senior status threat alone toward the risks GenAI poses to valued managerial outcomes such as accuracy, explainability, contextualization, and active user engagement.

## Key claims
- Junior professionals expected to educate senior professionals about effective GenAI use, but the main obstacle they described was not senior status threat; it was senior concern about GenAI risks to accuracy, explainability, contextualization, and users' active engagement with outputs.
- The authors identify three forms of novice AI risk mitigation tactics: recommendations grounded in limited understanding of GenAI capabilities, recommendations that overestimate changes to human routines rather than system design, and recommendations that overestimate project-level interventions rather than deployer- or ecosystem-level interventions.
- Juniors' limited understanding was plausible because the technology was nascent, fast-changing, accessible without coding or infrastructure ownership, and the juniors had no formal training, no work-setting experience, and little nonwork experience with it at the time of the study.
- The paper argues that emerging technology risks require attention to system design and deployer/ecosystem interventions, including reliable-use conditions, audit tools, prompt libraries, model alignment checks, onboarding, cognitive forcing functions, task-allocation design, and developer-level choices about model behavior.
- For AI adoption, the learning problem is not simply whether lower-status workers can teach higher-status workers; it is whether the would-be teachers have enough technical and organizational understanding to diagnose where human routine changes are insufficient.

## Evidence & limitations
- **Design:** qualitative interview study with 78 junior consultants at Boston Consulting Group in July-August 2023, conducted soon after participants used GPT-4 in a business problem-solving field experiment.
- **Evidence strength:** useful for theorizing how novice professionals reason about GenAI risk and senior-manager concerns in early adoption; the interviews were close to the task experience and anchored in a real consulting-work experiment.
- **Limitations:** single professional-services context, junior consultants only, early GPT-4 period, and evidence about recommended tactics rather than observed long-term coaching, adoption, or organizational learning outcomes.
