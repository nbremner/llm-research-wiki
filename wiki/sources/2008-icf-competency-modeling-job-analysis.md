# Competency Modeling & Job Analysis: Current trends and debates in the academic literature

**Citation.** ICF International. (2008). *Competency Modeling & Job Analysis: Current trends and debates in the academic literature.*

**Summary.** This practitioner report reviews the academic literature on competency modeling and job analysis, defining competencies, competency modeling, and task- and worker-oriented job analysis before summarizing trends and debates. Its central contribution is a boundary map: competency modeling and job analysis overlap substantially when both focus on job-level KSAOs, but they diverge when competency models operate at broader organizational or strategic levels.

## Key claims
- Competencies are typically defined as measurable KSAOs and other individual characteristics that differentiate performance, while competency modeling identifies and measures the KSAOs needed for successful job performance.
- Job analysis is divided into worker-oriented analysis, which identifies KSAOs needed for successful work, and task-oriented analysis, which documents what gets done on the job, how the job is performed, and the tools, information, people, and context involved.
- Technology-enabled job analysis and competency modeling can reduce cost and improve practicality through data warehouses, online SME focus groups, online surveys, and O*NET-based task information, but the report warns that applying average or pre-made profiles to specific jobs can introduce inaccuracy.
- Strategic job analysis shifts from describing current work to predicting future tasks, behaviors, and KSAOs; the report treats this as increasingly important for dynamic organizations but notes that empirical evidence was limited and largely prescriptive at the time.
- Cognitive task analysis is presented as a supplement to traditional task analysis for cognitively complex jobs; the report cites Lee's 2004 meta-analysis of 39 studies showing a large effect size of 1.70 and an estimated 35% performance gain, while noting lack of methodological consensus and high time/effort costs.
- Reliability evidence favors methodological rigor: incumbents tend to have lower job-analysis reliabilities than analysts or technical experts, task-specific data tend to be more reliable than generalized work data, and adding task statements plus frame-of-reference training can improve competency-modeling reliability, discriminant validity, and rating accuracy.
- The report argues against a single best method: the appropriate choice depends on purpose, organizational need, legal implications, environmental dynamism, desired specificity, and whether the unit of analysis is an individual job, multiple jobs, the organization, or cross-organizational core competencies.

## Evidence & limitations
- This is a short practitioner literature review from ICF International, publicly mirrored by the University of Baltimore, not a peer-reviewed empirical study.
- The report is useful for mapping constructs and debates around job analysis and competency modeling, especially for selection, training, succession planning, and future-oriented work analysis.
- The evidence base is dated to roughly 2008 and should not be treated as current on post-2008 competency modeling, O*NET practice, AI-mediated job analysis, or modern skills-taxonomy infrastructure.
- Several claims summarize cited research rather than reporting new data; the report's value is synthesis and practitioner framing, not original causal or validation evidence.
