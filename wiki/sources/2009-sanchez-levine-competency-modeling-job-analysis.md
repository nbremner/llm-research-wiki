# What is (or should be) the difference between competency modeling and traditional job analysis?

**Citation.** Sanchez, J. I., & Levine, E. L. (2009). What is (or should be) the difference between competency modeling and traditional job analysis? *Human Resource Management Review, 19*(2), 53–63. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.hrmr.2008.10.002

**Summary.** Sanchez and Levine argue that competency modeling and traditional job analysis should be treated as complementary HR tools with different purposes rather than interchangeable methods. Traditional job analysis describes current work and worker requirements, while competency modeling tries to influence role enactment by translating strategic priorities into day-to-day behavioral themes. The paper's core value for the wiki is its six-dimensional boundary map between describing work and steering strategically aligned behavior.

## Key claims
- Competency modeling fills a gap in traditional job analysis by injecting strategic concerns into everyday employee behavior, but it does not replace the rigor or job-specific grounding of traditional job analysis.
- The paper distinguishes traditional job analysis and competency modeling across six dimensions: purpose, view of the job, focus, time orientation, performance level, and measurement approach.
- Traditional job analysis primarily describes work assignments and relevant worker attributes, whereas competency modeling is meant to influence how employees enact their roles in alignment with organizational strategy.
- Traditional job analysis treats the job as an object to be described; competency modeling treats the job as a role interpreted and enacted by the employee, especially in service, information, and high-discretion work.
- Traditional job analysis is job-focused, past-oriented, and often incumbent-driven; competency modeling is organization-focused, future-oriented, and commonly driven by leaders responsible for strategy formulation and deployment.
- Competency labels may look too broad or behaviorally fuzzy from a latent-trait measurement perspective, but the authors argue that strategic competency language can be useful precisely because it communicates behavioral themes in business-relevant terms.
- The authors recommend cross-fertilization: strategic leaders should define broad competencies, while job-family SMEs and traditional job-analysis methods should translate those competencies into behavioral indicators that make strategic themes actionable in specific work contexts.

## Evidence & limitations
- This is a conceptual/review article in *Human Resource Management Review*, not an empirical validation study of a specific competency model.
- The article is strongest as a construct-boundary and method-fit source for distinguishing descriptive work analysis from prescriptive, strategy-linked competency modeling.
- The argument predates modern AI-mediated work analysis, skills taxonomies, and algorithmic inference of competencies, so its direct claims should be treated as foundational rather than current evidence on those systems.
- The authors explicitly acknowledge that the ideal distinction they propose is often blurred in field practice, where competency modeling and job analysis goals are frequently confounded.
