# AI receptivity

AI receptivity is the willingness, preference, or readiness to use AI products, services, or task execution. It is not the same construct as [[ai-literacy]]. Receptivity can come from informed confidence, convenience, social pressure, perceived usefulness, novelty, awe, or misunderstanding; the source of willingness matters for adoption quality.

[[2025-tully-ai-literacy-receptivity]] shows why this construct needs its own page. Across cross-country data and six preregistered studies, lower AI literacy often predicted greater AI receptivity. The proposed mechanism was magical perception and awe when AI appeared to perform tasks associated with distinctly human attributes, rather than stronger belief in AI capability or lower fear of AI.

That distinction matters for AI adoption. High receptivity is not automatically good. It may indicate openness to useful experimentation, but it may also signal anthropomorphic over-trust, poor calibration, or consumer-facing appeal built on mystification. Adoption work therefore needs to ask not only whether people are receptive, but why.

## Connections
- Relates to [[ai-adoption]] because receptivity can precede trial, usage, and organizational adoption, but does not by itself show durable work-system change.
- Relates to [[human-ai-collaboration]] because willingness to involve AI does not guarantee calibrated delegation, verification, or accountability.
- Relates to [[automation-complacency]] because receptivity driven by awe or magical thinking may increase acceptance without increasing oversight.
- Relates to [[construct-validity]] because receptivity measures can conflate intention, preference for AI over humans, past use, perceived usefulness, trust, and readiness.

## Contradictions & open questions
- [[2025-tully-ai-literacy-receptivity]] complicates the assumption that more AI knowledge produces more willingness to use AI; in some domains, mystification may increase receptivity.
- Receptivity may reverse by task framing. For tasks associated with shared rather than distinctly human attributes, higher AI literacy can predict greater AI receptivity.
- Receptivity is not adoption quality. Greater willingness to use AI could mean useful exploration, overreliance, susceptibility to anthropomorphic marketing, or premature delegation depending on stakes and governance.
- The workplace version remains underdeveloped: consumer or student receptivity may not translate into sustained AI use inside accountable jobs, teams, or organizations.
