# AI readiness

AI readiness is the organizational capacity to make a specific AI adoption purpose viable. [[2021-johnk-ready-or-not-ai-comes]] makes the construct purpose- and context-specific: readiness is not a generic maturity score or a one-time gate before adoption. Because AI is a general-purpose technology, organizations have to assess readiness against the particular use case, work process, customer interface, and adoption stage they are trying to move through.

Jöhnk, Weißert, and Wyrtki organize readiness into five categories: **strategic alignment**, **resources**, **knowledge**, **culture**, and **data**. The factors are concrete enough to be useful diagnostically — AI-business potentials, customer AI readiness, top-management support, AI-process fit, data-driven decision-making, financial budget, personnel, IT infrastructure, AI awareness, upskilling, AI ethics, innovativeness, collaborative work, change management, data availability, data quality, data accessibility, and data flow — but the paper treats the indicators as illustrative rather than validated measures.

The important move for this wiki is that readiness and adoption are mutually reinforcing. Readiness makes successful adoption more likely, but successful smaller-scale adoption also builds readiness by producing experience, acceptance, and clearer understanding of where AI can create value. That makes readiness a dynamic design problem, not a static compliance checklist.

[[2026-reich-work-design-multidimensional-ai-threat]] adds a job-design boundary to readiness. Even if organizational knowledge, culture, and resources are present, adoption may still vary by whether roles provide autonomy and skill variety for experimentation. Readiness therefore has a work-design layer below the organization-level checklist.

[[2025-tona-deployment-ai-infer-employee-skills]] adds a workforce-capability layer to readiness. J&J's case treats digital-transformation readiness as partly a problem of knowing which skills the current workforce has, which future capabilities the organization values, and whether AI-inferred skill data can support reskilling and internal-mobility decisions. Readiness therefore includes the measurement and governance capacity to make workforce capability visible without turning employee data into an unvalidated or untrusted control system.

## Connections

- Connects to [[ai-adoption]] because readiness is one way of naming what binds adoption after tools become available: not access, but organizational capability, commitment, data, and fit for the intended adoption purpose.
- Connects to [[work-redesign]] through AI-process fit, data flow, and the need to reshape work processes before AI systems can produce reliable value.
- Connects to [[ai-mediated-work-experience]] because readiness is partly experienced through autonomy, upskilling, workload, identity threat, and local support for experimentation.
- Connects to [[construct-validity]] if readiness factors become survey or assessment instruments: the paper proposes factors and indicators, but future work still has to validate whether those indicators coherently measure readiness.
- Connects to [[human-ai-collaboration]] through customer/user readiness, upskilling, AI awareness, and AI ethics — adoption quality depends on humans understanding, accepting, and governing AI-enabled work.
- Connects to [[competency-modeling]] because readiness for digital transformation may require a future-oriented skills architecture before upskilling, mobility, or AI deployment can be targeted coherently.

## Contradictions & open questions

- [[2021-johnk-ready-or-not-ai-comes]] frames readiness broadly across organizational factors, while [[2026-kim-mapping-ai-into-production]] isolates a narrower causal bottleneck: managerial discovery of where AI creates value. The relationship between broad readiness assessments and high-leverage mapping interventions is open.
- The proposed 58 indicators are illustrative, not psychometrically validated. If AI readiness becomes an organizational assessment construct, the measurement work still has to establish dimensionality, reliability, criterion validity, and incremental validity over existing innovation/adoption readiness constructs.
- Readiness may be partly endogenous to adoption. If successful pilots build readiness, then cross-sectional readiness scores could confuse preconditions with outcomes unless timing is modeled carefully.
- [[2026-reich-work-design-multidimensional-ai-threat]] raises the same timing problem at the job level: autonomy and skill variety may enable AI adoption, be perceived differently by adopters, or both.
- [[2025-tona-deployment-ai-infer-employee-skills]] raises a measurement-governance tension: AI skills inference can make readiness more visible, but readiness claims become fragile if inferred skills are treated as more accurate, complete, or employee-accepted than the evidence supports.
