# Employee change support

Employee change support names the difference between workers who minimally comply with organizational change and workers who actively champion it. [[2018-fugate-change-compliance-championing]] is useful for AI transformation because it treats support for change as more than resistance reduction: compliance can keep implementation moving, but championing requires discretionary effort, initiative, problem solving, and social promotion of the change to others.

The mechanism is appraisal-based. In a two-wave merger field study, change management support — useful information, clarity about mutual expectations, and trust in management competence — predicted lower threat appraisal and higher challenge appraisal. Challenge appraisal then predicted both compliance and championing, while threat appraisal predicted lower championing but not lower compliance. For AI transformation, that implies leaders cannot infer commitment from surface compliance: employees may follow instructions while still withholding the discretionary sensemaking, repair work, and peer advocacy that make AI-enabled change spread.

Dispositional resistance matters, but not in the fatalistic way leaders often assume. [[2018-fugate-change-compliance-championing]] found that change management support had a stronger positive relationship with challenge appraisal among employees higher in dispositional resistance to change. The practical read is not "resistant people block transformation"; it is that clear, trustworthy, competent change management may matter most for the employees most likely to interpret disruption as costly or unsettling.

## Connections
- Connects to [[ai-adoption]] because durable AI adoption depends on employees doing more than privately trying tools or complying with rollout instructions; championing is one route by which local use becomes socially visible, legitimate, and transferable.
- Connects to [[behavioral-human-centered-ai]] because appraisal, threat, trust, autonomy, and resistance are behavioral mechanisms that shape whether AI change is accepted, rejected, or actively promoted.
- Connects to [[ai-readiness]] because readiness includes the organizational capability to provide credible information, expectation clarity, and managerial competence during disruption, not only data, infrastructure, skills, and strategic alignment.
- Connects to [[organizational-learning-capability]] because employee championing can help local lessons travel, while mere compliance may leave experimentation isolated and fragile.
- Connects to [[ai-mediated-work-experience]] because employees appraise AI-enabled change through expected gains, losses, control, workload, identity, and competence, not only through formal business-case logic.

## Contradictions & open questions
- [[2018-fugate-change-compliance-championing]] is a merger study, not an AI implementation study. The appraisal mechanism is highly relevant, but AI transformations may add distinct threats around surveillance, autonomy, skill value, identity, and algorithmic opacity that need direct field evidence.
- Compliance can look like adoption in dashboards. If AI rollout metrics count access, usage, training completion, or policy adherence, they may miss whether employees are championing, repairing, adapting, or socially legitimating the change.
- Threat appraisal did not significantly predict compliance in the merger study, possibly because high unemployment made compliance necessary even for threatened employees. In AI transformation, the same pattern may appear when workers comply to avoid seeming obsolete, disloyal, or non-adaptable while still withholding discretionary effort.
- Championing is not automatically good. Employees can champion poorly governed AI systems, overstate benefits, or pressure peers into harmful adoption; support for change still has to be bounded by [[responsible-ai-deployment]] and evidence quality.
