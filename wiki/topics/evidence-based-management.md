# Evidence-based management

Evidence-based management asks whether organizational claims are supported by the best available,
relevant evidence — and whether the evidence is strong enough for the decision being made.

[[2014-briner-employee-engagement-evidence-based]] applies that stance to employee engagement. Briner
argues that the most important practical questions are causal: whether engagement increases lead to
performance increases, and whether engagement interventions increase engagement and then performance.
Cross-sectional correlations cannot answer those questions, especially when the construct itself is
unclear and measures overlap with older attitudes.

This is directly relevant to AI workforce transformation. Claims about AI adoption, productivity,
well-being, skill atrophy, or human-AI collaboration need evidence matched to the claim: descriptive
usage data can show uptake, but not causal impact; correlations can suggest associations, but not prove
intervention effects.

## Connections

- Relates to [[construct-validity]] because evidence quality depends on whether the measured construct
  is coherent enough to interpret.
- Relates to [[employee-engagement]] as a practitioner domain where advocacy often outran evidence.
- Relates to [[ai-adoption]] because adoption interventions will need designs capable of separating
  access, discovery, workflow redesign, and performance effects.

## Contradictions & open questions

- Evidence-based management should discipline claims without becoming paralysis. The wiki should track
  where imperfect evidence is still decision-relevant and where claims are too weak to operationalize.
- AI transformation evidence will often be fast-moving and proprietary; the public-only boundary means
  the wiki may underrepresent some internal organizational evidence even when such evidence exists.
